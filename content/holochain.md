Are non-fungible tokens the most important thing in cryptocurrency history? They appear to be larger than DeFi, Hip Hop, and Hollywood. We'll have to wait and see. However, the promise of tracking ownership of items indefinitely without the use of centralized intermediaries has piqued people's interest. And, in many cases, their money: NFT sales totaled nearly $2.5 billion in the first half of 2021, with some tokens fetching millions of dollars each.

Concerning Fungibility

Fungible items are interchangeable with other instances of the same item:

A soup can is nearly interchangeable with other soup cans from the same batch.
A new tennis racket can be used interchangeably with other new rackets of the same manufacture and type.
A currency's units are designed to be convertible with other currencies' units.

Real-world items, on the other hand, are rarely fungible:

An acre of land cannot be substituted for another acre of land. This is why title records rely on lengthy property descriptions that include latitudes, longitudes, landmarks, and other surveyor notes.
Similar houseplants aren't interchangeable with your favorite.
Despite what our Facebook stats might suggest, friends aren't replaceable.

Because blockchains were created to manage replaceable units of cash in the first place, there is no built-in functionality to make units unique. When Alice spends 10 Bitcoin from her wallet, there's no way to tell if the units she's spending came from Bob, Carol, or anywhere else. It's like scooping water into a bucket: you don't know which inbound scoop of water the molecules you're getting originated from when you scoop some out.

So, in order to produce a unique representation of something, a new layer must be added: smart contracts that uniquely identify assets and keep NFTs separate as ownership is transferred. The goal is to ensure that a token isn't simply a representation of a work of art.
